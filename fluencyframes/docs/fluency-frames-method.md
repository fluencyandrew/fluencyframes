# FluencyFrames — Method, Learning Flow & Course Entities
### Single Source of Truth — Pedagogical Specification

---

## 1. What FluencyFrames Is

FluencyFrames is not a language-learning app in the conventional sense. It is a **fluency automation engine** for B2–C1+ professionals who already communicate in English but have plateaued — who can understand almost everything but whose production under pressure does not yet match their comprehension or their professional standing.

The problem it solves is not a knowledge problem. It is an **automation problem**. The gap between what a learner knows and what they can produce instantly, precisely, and fluently in a high-stakes meeting, negotiation, or boardroom Q&A is a failure of retrieval speed and neural consolidation — not of vocabulary size. FluencyFrames is built to close that gap specifically.

The experience should never feel like a quiz or a language course. It should feel like being inside a realistic professional situation and having to communicate precisely, with the right language already at your fingertips.

---

## 2. Core Pedagogical Framework — The 3 Rs

Every exercise, every session, and every content decision in FluencyFrames is built around a single cognitive framework describing how language moves from conscious knowledge to automatic production.

### R1 — Rumination
Deep semantic processing. The learner encounters language in a meaningful professional context and must think carefully about which word or phrase best fits the communicative goal of the situation. This is not guessing — it is deliberate discrimination between semantically close options where only one is rhetorically optimal. No time pressure. The goal is to build strong initial meaning connections and situational anchoring.

### R2 — Re-exposure
The same language is revisited across varied contexts, collocates, and grammatical frames. Each re-encounter reshapes and strengthens the neural trace, making retrieval faster and more flexible. The learner is required to produce the word from memory with partial scaffolding — a first letter, a collocate clue, or part of the grammatical pattern. Soft time pressure may apply.

### R3 — Repetition
The learner speaks the target word or phrase aloud under time pressure. Automatic retrieval, physical production, pronunciation accuracy, and stress pattern are all trained simultaneously. A hard countdown (6–8 seconds) simulates the retrieval demand of real-time professional speech. This is where automation is built — where language moves from effortful recall to fluent, unconscious output.

The spacing intervals between R1, R2, and R3 exposures follow a spaced repetition schedule aligned with memory consolidation windows: +24h, +48h, +72h recurring, with longer intervals (+7d, +14d, +28d) for deeply embedded items. An incorrect answer resets the interval to 24h.

## 3. What a Learner Is Building

The learner is not completing exercises. They are **constructing a lexical knowledge graph** — a personal, growing network of words, word families, grammatical patterns, collocations, and semantic relationships that expands continuously throughout the programme.

Every word encountered becomes a permanent node in this graph. Every collocation, pattern, and semantic connection encountered between words becomes an edge. Over months, the learner can see this graph grow — from a sparse cluster of related words in Week 1 to a dense, interconnected network of professionally relevant language by the end of the 52-week programme.

This shifts the psychology of learning from task completion to knowledge construction. The learner is not finishing exercises. They are building something.

## 4. The Vocabulary Architecture

### 4.1 The Primary Learning Unit — The Word Family

The primary unit of learning in FluencyFrames is the **word family** — a root form together with all its morphological variants. Learning *product* means learning *product*, *products*, *production*, *productive*, *productivity*, *productively*, and *produce* as a connected family, not as isolated items.

In the lexical cluster panel (Layer 2 of the exercise screen), word families are always displayed by their **root form**. This means a learner choosing between *efficient*, *economical*, and *productive* as answer options will see the roots *effici-*, *econom-*, and *product-* displayed in the cluster, each with a number indicating how many forms in that family exist in the programme. The ring around each root shows how much of the complete family has been learned.

Tapping a root form in the cluster opens a **family dropdown** showing all its forms individually, each with a progress bar showing that specific form's exposure count. Forms the learner already knows from pre-programme life are shown in grey. Programme forms not yet encountered are shown dimly with a dashed border. Forms in progress and mastered forms are shown in the POS colour system (see section 4.3).

A word family is considered fully learned only when all its programme forms have reached the mastery threshold.

### 4.2 How Vocabulary Is Planned — Collocations First

Vocabulary is not selected arbitrarily from frequency lists. The planning approach works backwards from **collocations**, because collocations are how fluent speakers actually store and retrieve language. A word learned as part of *unforeseen circumstances* or *operational disruption* has immediate situational grip — a cognitive anchor that makes both words easier to retrieve and easier to use correctly.

The planning process follows three tiers:

**Tier 1 — Theme-driven collocations.** Each story arc has a professional theme. Collocations are harvested from Cambridge Collocations in Use by theme first, giving the curriculum its situational vocabulary. Each collocation yields two words simultaneously — both enter the programme.

**Tier 2 — Backtracking from collocating words.** Every collocating word is then examined independently: what does *unforeseen* collocate with across different contexts? This backtracking is recursive and generates the broader vocabulary network organically, with words earning their place through genuine collocational productivity rather than arbitrary selection.

**Tier 3 — Academic Word List supplementation.** The AWL (Coxhead) fills the remaining 50% of programme vocabulary, prioritising sublists 1–6 which cover the most professionally relevant general academic vocabulary. AWL words that emerge naturally through Tier 1–2 backtracking are already covered; those that do not are introduced in general professional contexts across the 52-week arc.

### 4.3 Part of Speech Colour Coding

All language in FluencyFrames is colour-coded by part of speech. This applies to answer option pills, passive highlights in the message thread, root nodes and word family forms in the cluster, collocate tags, and the blank itself once filled. The palette is drawn from the existing FluencyFrames design system:

- **Nouns** — cyan `#00e5ff`
- **Verbs** — purple `#7b61ff`
- **Adjectives** — lilac `#a78bfa`

This colour system is not decorative. It encodes grammatical information that is pedagogically relevant — particularly for pattern grammar (see section 7), where the grammatical category of each slot in a pattern determines which form of a word is required.

### 4.4 Semantic Relationships in the Cluster

The lexical cluster in Layer 2 shows the focus word's root surrounded by semantically related roots. Three types of relationship are represented:

**Semantic** — near-synonyms in the same cluster (e.g. *disrupt-* / *interrupt-* / *set back-*). These are words close enough to be confused but distinct enough that the choice between them is the lesson being taught in Layer 1.

**Hypernym** — a broader, simpler, already-known word that shares a key semantic component with the target (e.g. *break* as a hypernym of *disrupt-*). These are known anchor words displayed with a plain grey border and no progress ring — they are not programme targets but provide a cognitive foothold for discriminating the meaning of the target.

**Collocate** — words that naturally co-occur with the target across different grammatical slots (e.g. *significant* collocates with *disrupt-* as a modifier). These cross-cluster connections are what make the graph feel like a real language system rather than a collection of synonym groups.

## 5. The Collocation System

Collocations are not a separate feature — they are the primary mechanism by which new vocabulary is embedded and re-encountered. Every target word is introduced in the context of at least one high-frequency collocation, and its subsequent R2 and R3 exposures vary the collocating partner to build flexible retrieval across different collocational slots.

In the cluster panel, collocations for the selected root form are displayed alongside the family dropdown. Up to six collocations are shown regardless of whether the learner has yet encountered them — they represent the full productive range of the word. Each collocate is displayed in its POS colour, so *finished product* shows *finished* in lilac and *product* in cyan, *boost productivity* shows *boost* in purple and *productivity* in cyan, and so on.

Collocations have their own progression. Once individual words are sufficiently embedded, they begin appearing inside **collocation-level exercises** where the learner must choose or produce the precise collocating partner, not just the headword. This is how the curriculum moves from single-word precision to chunk-level fluency.

## 6. The Daily Session

### 6.1 Structure

Each daily session consists of **25 exercises**, taking approximately 25 minutes to complete. The target is roughly 10 new words introduced and 15 returning words reviewed, though the exact split is managed by the spaced repetition engine based on which words are due.

The session opens with a **warmup**: a replay of yesterday's conversation thread with audio, where the user fills in only the brand-new vocabulary from the previous day. Returning vocabulary from earlier days is shown passively in the thread — the user reads it but is not tested on it. This warmup exploits the episodic memory of recent situational context (which fades within approximately 48 hours) to make the new vocabulary feel embedded in a scene the learner genuinely remembers. The warmup is not scored and carries no time pressure.

### 6.2 Mastery Threshold

A word is considered **learned** after 12 exposures of any type, with the proviso that at least one exposure must come from each of the three modes (R1, R2, R3) before mastery is confirmed. Exposure types and their weights:

- R1 correct selection: +1.0
- R2 correct typed retrieval: +1.0
- R3 correct spoken retrieval: +1.0
- Passive highlight (seen but not tested): +0.5
- Unchosen option in R1 (seen as a distractor): +0.5
- Incorrect answer: −0.5 (floor of zero)

A word family is considered fully learned only when all its programme forms have individually reached the mastery threshold.

### 6.3 Session Completion

After the 25th exercise, a **session summary screen** appears showing: exercises completed (25/25), words that progressed today with before-and-after exposure counts, total words mastered to date, and the current streak. This creates completion psychology and makes progress visible on a daily basis.

## 7. Grammar Patterns

Patterns are drawn from **Collins COBUILD Pattern Grammar** and are an integral part of how language is tested and presented from the beginning of the programme. Every target word in an exercise is associated with one or more patterns that describe the grammatical frames in which it commonly appears.

Patterns are named using standard pattern notation. For example, the pattern `V n against n` describes a verb used transitively with two noun objects linked by *against* — as in *weigh the advantages against the costs*. The pattern `V n` describes a simple transitive verb. The pattern `it V-s that` describes a subject-it construction — as in *it emerged that the report had been misfiled*.

Understanding the pattern is inseparable from understanding the word, because many near-synonyms diverge precisely at the level of their grammatical frames. *Arise* takes `V` (intransitive) while *raise* takes `V n` (transitive) — a learner who conflates them will produce errors not at the vocabulary level but at the structural level. Patterns make this distinction explicit and testable.

## 8. The Three Exercise Types

All three exercise types share the same visual container. The top portion of the screen shows the narrative context — the story arc, the interlocutor, the message thread, and the communicative goal. The bottom portion shows the lexical cluster. What changes across the three types is the **input demand** placed on the learner and the **information provided** to support retrieval.

### Exercise Type 1 — Guided Selection (R1 · Rumination)

**Layer 1 (exercise area):** The target sentence is displayed with a blank. The communicative goal is shown above. The learner selects the correct answer from 2–4 horizontal pill options. All options are programme words from the same semantic cluster — none is grammatically wrong or broadly semantically impossible, only one is rhetorically optimal for the stated communicative goal and situation. The relevant grammatical pattern is **highlighted with a muted background colour** in the exercise sentence, and the pattern name is displayed above the sentence (e.g. `V n`). The blank and the pattern together orient the learner to the grammatical slot being filled.

**Layer 2 (lexical cluster):** Shows the root form of the selected option at the centre, with its semantic neighbours arranged around it. The pattern associated with the correct answer is highlighted in the cluster panel, with the pattern name displayed above it. The learner can tap any root node to compare its family forms and collocations. No target word is hidden — all options remain visible as cluster nodes.

After confirming the answer: feedback text explains why the chosen word is correct (or why the correct word was preferable), with specific reference to the communicative goal, register, semantic distinction, and collocational behaviour.

### Exercise Type 2 — Assisted Retrieval (R2 · Re-exposure)

**Layer 1 (exercise area):** The sentence is displayed with the blank, but the **pattern** is now partially embedded in the sentence around the blank. The learner sees part of the pattern's frame already filled in — for example, in a `V n against n` exercise, the learner might see *I really think we need to weigh ___ ___ ___ the minuses* and must produce *the pluses against*. The pattern name is displayed above. The learner types the answer into the blank. A first-letter hint or collocate clue may be available. A soft timer applies.

This type tests not only the target word but the learner's ability to recall the correct **form** of the word (e.g. converting *plus* to *pluses*) and the **pattern frame** in which it sits. The two demands are inseparable — producing the right word in the wrong grammatical frame is a production error even if the vocabulary is correct.

**Layer 2 (lexical cluster):** Identical to Exercise Type 1 — the root form of the target word is centred, surrounded by its semantic neighbours. The pattern is highlighted. The target word **is visible** in the cluster as usual. The learner can compare neighbours and consult collocations before or after answering.

### Exercise Type 3 — Free Recall (R3 · Repetition)

**Layer 1 (exercise area):** The sentence is displayed with the blank. The pattern name is shown above. A hard countdown of 6–8 seconds begins. The learner speaks the answer aloud. Speech recognition confirms whether the correct word was produced. Pronunciation accuracy and stress pattern are evaluated. No options are shown. No scaffolding is provided.

**Layer 2 (lexical cluster):** The target word is **removed** from the cluster. The learner sees only its semantic neighbours — the surrounding words whose meaning and collocational behaviour they already know — but must retrieve the centre word independently from context and from the relational memory of the cluster's shape. The pattern is still visible and named, providing structural orientation without lexical retrieval support.

This is the fullest test of automation: the learner must retrieve the precise word, in the correct form, within the correct grammatical frame, under time pressure, with only contextual and relational cues available. This is the condition that mirrors genuine high-stakes professional speech.

## 9. The Lexical Cluster — Layer 2 in Detail

The lexical cluster is permanently visible at the bottom of the exercise screen throughout every session. It is not a popup and not an afterthought — it is the learning layer that gives every exercise its pedagogical depth.

### 9.1 What It Shows

The cluster centres on the **root form** of the word currently in focus — either the word being tested in the blank or the word the user has selected as a candidate answer. Around it are arranged 4–6 semantic neighbours, also displayed as root forms.

Each node in the cluster has one of four visual states:

**Known anchor** — a word the learner already knew before the programme (A1–B1 level). Displayed with a plain grey solid border, dim text, no progress ring. These words provide cognitive footholds for discriminating the meaning of programme words — they are reference points, not targets.

**Programme, unseen** — a word that exists in the programme but has not yet been encountered by this learner. Displayed with a dashed border, very dim text, no ring fill. These nodes represent visible future territory — the learner can see what is coming.

**Programme, in progress** — a word the learner has encountered and is actively building. The border is a conic-gradient progress ring in the word's POS colour, filling clockwise in proportion to the number of exposures completed across the full word family. Text is at normal brightness in the POS colour.

**Programme, mastered** — a word family fully learned across all forms and patterns. The border ring is complete and glows softly. Text is at full POS colour brightness.

### 9.2 The Root Node and Family Dropdown

Tapping any root node opens a **family dropdown** listing all the programme forms of that word family. Each form appears with its POS colour and a small exposure progress bar to the right. Grey forms are pre-programme known words. Collocations for the root are shown to the right of the family list — up to six, always displayed regardless of discovery status, each collocate word in its POS colour.

The family dropdown answers the question: *how much of this word do I actually know?* A learner might have 12 exposures to *disrupt* as a verb but zero exposures to *disruption* as a noun — the dropdown makes this asymmetry visible and motivating.

### 9.3 What the Cluster Is Not

The cluster is not a dictionary. It does not define words. It shows relationships — which words are semantically adjacent, which are broader anchors, which are narrower variants, and how much of each a learner has built. The learner constructs meaning through repeated navigation of these relationships across exercises, not through explicit definitions.

## 10. The Story System

### 10.1 One Arc, Seven Situations

Each week revolves around **one ongoing professional narrative** involving the learner as themselves. The arc advances daily across seven distinct situations:

- Monday: Client escalation
- Tuesday: Internal conflict
- Wednesday: Supplier or partner negotiation
- Thursday: Leadership presentation
- Friday: Deadline crisis
- Saturday: Team disagreement
- Sunday: Cross-cultural misunderstanding

These situations are written to be **role-agnostic but stakes-specific**. A missed deadline, a difficult client, a negotiation under pressure — these scenarios are universally recognisable across industries and seniority levels without requiring personalisation. The learner brings their professional identity to the situation; the situation provides the communicative pressure.

### 10.2 Recurring Characters

Interlocutors recur across the programme. The learner builds a professional relationship with them over weeks. Early exercises with a character feel like introductions; later exercises feel like navigating an established, complex relationship with history. This character continuity deepens the episodic memory anchoring of vocabulary encountered in each arc.

### 10.3 The Warmup and Episodic Memory

Each session opens with a replay of yesterday's conversation thread with audio. The learner reads back through the exchange — the full message thread including both the interlocutor's messages and their own responses — and fills in only the words that were brand new yesterday. Words from earlier in the programme appear passively in the thread, visible but not tested.

The reason for this design is specific: episodic memory of a professional situation fades within approximately 48 hours, but within that window it provides a powerful retrieval cue for vocabulary encountered inside it. The warmup exploits this window deliberately — the learner remembers the scene, the stakes, and the character, and this situational memory cues the target vocabulary with far more force than a decontextualised flashcard ever could.

### 10.4 The User as Themselves

The learner always plays themselves. Onboarding captures their name, gender, and professional role. They are given an avatar that represents them — either a photo they upload or a generated initial-based avatar. In every exercise, they see themselves as the protagonist of the professional situation. This is not cosmetic. It increases the perceived stakes of communicative choices and deepens the episodic memory anchoring of vocabulary encountered in each scene.

## 11. Mastery, Progress and Completion

A **word family** is considered learned when all its programme forms have each accumulated 12 qualifying exposures across all three modes. A **collocation** is considered learned when it has been correctly produced or selected in context at least three times.

The learner can see their progress in three places:

In the **header** throughout every session, showing total words in the programme learned to date and the current day's session progress (exercises completed out of 25).

In the **lexical cluster** throughout every exercise, where the progress rings on each node give a real-time sense of the network's density and the learner's position within it.

In the **session summary** at the end of each session, showing daily and cumulative progress, streak, and a selection of words that advanced during the session.

Over the 52-week programme, these indicators create a visible, tangible record of language development — not a test score, but a growing knowledge graph that the learner owns.

## 12. The 52-Week Curriculum

The programme is divided into four phases across 12 modules:

**Phase 1 — Precision (Weeks 1–13):** Lexical discrimination between near-synonyms. The learner develops the ability to choose the precise word for a given communicative goal. Focus is on noun clusters, verb clusters, and the adjectival precision that separates functional from authoritative English. Situations are professional but broadly accessible.

**Phase 2 — Stance (Weeks 14–26):** Language for managing position, hedging, conceding, asserting, and framing. The learner develops control over register and epistemic distance — knowing not just what to say but how committed or cautious to sound when saying it. Patterns become central as modal and reporting structures are introduced.

**Phase 3 — Influence (Weeks 27–39):** Rhetorical and persuasive language. The learner develops the vocabulary and structures needed to frame arguments, manage objections, and shape how information is received. Negotiation, presentation, and escalation scenarios dominate.

**Phase 4 — Register and Texture (Weeks 40–52):** Refinement at the level of texture, tone, and discourse structure. The learner develops awareness of how word choice, sentence rhythm, and structural pattern combine to create the impression of executive-level fluency. Collocational range and idiomatic precision are the primary targets.

The target lexical range across all four phases is approximately 2,500 word families, learned through their most productive collocations, embedded in seven recurring situational arc types, and tested through three progressive exercise modes.

---

## 13. What FluencyFrames Is Building Toward

At the end of the programme, a learner has not completed a course. They have built a personal professional lexicon — a knowledge graph containing thousands of interconnected words, word families, patterns, and collocations, each embedded in situational memory, each retrievable under pressure, each available at the speed that professional communication demands.

The test of success is not a score or a certificate. It is what happens in the next negotiation, the next boardroom presentation, the next Q&A after a difficult quarter. The language is there. It comes without searching. That is fluency — not as a concept, but as a physical fact of how the brain retrieves language. FluencyFrames is engineered to produce exactly that.
